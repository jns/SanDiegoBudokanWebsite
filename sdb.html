About the San Diego Budōkan

San Diego Budōkan first opened its doors in 1968. In the five decades since it's founding hundreds of men and women have crossed the threshold of the dōjō and been transformed by the powerful teachings. The traditions we study are not mere shells or archaic relics, but powerful living traditions that focus the spirit and sharpen the mind. Our goal as disciples of these traditions is to penetrate to the core of conflict and strategy, and to emerge with the capability to act instantly and decisively with what is precisely required by the situation.

We study two Japanese traditions: Ittō Tenshin-ryū® Kenjutsu and Yamate-ryū® Aikijutsu. These traditions require strict discipline and formality, and their study is a life-long endeavor. Students are expected to train a minimum of two times a week, are required to wear a training uniform, and will need to purchase the appropriate weapons and training tools.

We additionally offer a no gi jujutsu class. This is not Brazilian jiu-jitsu. The no gi jujutsu class exposes students to small subset of very practical teachings from the Japanese traditions. Class focuses on building conditioned responses to stressful and dangerous situations. Students dress in every day clothes and class will include drills to develop balance, sensitivity, and awareness. Students will practice realistic scenarios and learn to act decisively, with intention and ferocity.

Our Traditions

Ittō Tenshin-ryū®

Ittō Tenshin-ryū was founded by Kurosawa Kojiro (circa 1900) and brought to the United States in the 1960s by the third-generation headmaster, Mr. Fredrick Lovret. The current headmaster is Mr. Arvind Rajguru. By the time the Ittō Tenshin-ryū was created, the major wars leading to the unification of Japan had been over for almost three hundred years. In keeping with the times, the techniques of the style were oriented more toward personal combat than mass engagements. The history of the Ittō Tenshin-ryō is not well documented prior to the founder of the tradition, or the wellspring of his training. According to our oral traditions, the heart of the art descends from the Ittō-ryu through the Tenshin Ittō-ryō.

The purpose of the Ittō Tenshin-ryō is not to perpetuate a set of physical techniques or to preserve an archaic method of combat, but to develop a powerful and orderly being. Our methodology of training is short, simple techniques combined with vigorous training.

Four Essential Principles of the Itto Tenshin-ryu:

  • Techniques are to be done in a natural and fluid manner.
  • Techniques shall be large and open. Small techniques are for small spirits.
  • The development of a powerful and orderly being is of primary importance.
  • There is no defense; only attack, Attack, ATTACK!

Yamate-ryū®

The Yamate-ryū began as a family martial art. It is a direct descendant of the Daito-ryū and remains oriented toward its original purpose.

In the early years of the twentieth century, Takeda Sogaku, a master swordsman of the Ono-ha Itto-ryu, was teaching his family art, the Daito-ryu, to certain influential people in northern Japan. Takeda never had a dojo; he traveled from place to place giving seminars. Some of these seminars were for individuals, others were to the hand-to-hand combat instructors of police departments and military organizations, who later taught the art to members of their units. During this period, he served as an instructor to the 2nd Sendai Army Regiment in north-eastern Honshu.

Membership
